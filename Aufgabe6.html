Excersise 6 - Heated Room#

This Report shall include a thermal analysis of a room heated by a single Radiator. In the first part of the analysis, the mechanical fluid problem of the air inside the room will be solved through the use of the openFOAM [6] library. In the second part, the heat loss and the comfort criteria defined in the EN-ISO-7730 Standard [2] is analyzed.

Problem Description#

Geometrie#

The problem consists of a 6 m by 5 m by 2.6 m room, with two neighboring exterior walls and two neighboring interior walls. The 1 m by 1 m by 0.30 m radiator is positioned in the middle of the longer external Wall. The Situation is shown in Fig. 7.

Boundary Conditions#

The analyzed room represents a room on the ground floor with neighboring rooms on two sides and above. The Problem is defined by four Dirichlet Boundary Conditions:

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} &\text{Outside Air Temperature:}& & T_a = -10°C& \\ &\text{Ground Temperature:}& & T_g = 5°C& \\ &\text{Neighbouring Room Air Temperature:}& & T_{i,n}=20°C& \\ &\text{Radiator Surface Temperature:}& & T_{r}=55°C& \\ \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Analysis#

To simplify the exercise, an example of the room is provided. This section describes how the provided example was altered to fit the described Problem.

Software#

The Problem is analyzed using the provided openFOAM Docker image: opencfd/openfoam-run:2306.

Mesh#

The Mesh is created in two steps. In the first step, a mesh of Hexahedrons is created using blockMesh. To improve the quality of the solution in the corners, a mesh refinement is implemented in the following divisions:

(0.2 0.3 8) //Division 1
(0.6 0.40 1) //Division 2
(0.2 0.3 0.125) //Division 3

The Grading is applied in each direction (x,y,z). To account for the complex geometry of the heater, further mesh refinement is applied using snappyHexMesh. Around the radiator, a refinement box with 2 x 0.5 x 2 m is applied. To smoothen the transition, three distances of mesh refinement are defined:

  • 0-15 cm distance: 3 levels

  • 15-30 cm distance: 2 levels

  • 30-60 cm distance: 1 level

To reduce processing time a minimum mesh size is ensured by setting minRefinementCells 400. The Resulting Mesh is shown in Fig. 8 as a cross-section through the radiator.

Solver#

As a solver, the buoyantSimpleFoam from the openFOAM standard solvers is chosen, as in addition to solving buoyant, turbulent flow, it takes radiative heat transfer into account. To improve to quality of the results, the residuals are reduced as follows:

    p_rgh           1e-2;     -->     p_rgh           1e-4;
    U               1e-3;     -->     U               1e-5;
    h               1e-3;     -->     h               1e-5;
    G               1e-3;     -->     G               1e-5;

To increase the rate of convergence the releaxiation factors are increased as follows:

    U               0.2;     -->     U               0.7;
    h               0.2;     -->     h               0.7;
    "(k|epsilon|R)" 0.5;     -->     "(k|epsilon|R)" 0.5;
    G               0.7;     -->     G               0.7;

Turbulance Model#

As the vortex field itself is of no particular interest in this exercise, the RASModel kEpsilon is chosen as the turbulence Model.

Radiation Model#

As a Radiation Model, the viewFactor*-Model is chosen. In Contrast to the opaqueSolid Model, the absorption of radiation by the air is neglected. In other words, only interaction between surfaces is considered. To further reduce processing time, the interaction of surfaces with a view angle lower than 10° is neglected.

    extWork         0;
    relHumidity     60;

Results#

Analytical Solution#

Simplifying the airmass in the room as a node, the Problem can be solved using the RC-Network described in Exercise 2.

#Libraries
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

class node:
    # n summarizes the number of all nodes
    n=0
    def __init__(self, number,location):
        self.number = number
        self.location = location
        node.n += 1

class conduction:
    def __init__(self, fromNode, toNode, conductance):
        self.fromNode = fromNode
        self.toNode = toNode
        self.K = conductance
        

class boundary:
    def __init__(self, toNode, temp, conductance):
        self.toNode = toNode
        self.temp = temp
        self.K = conductance

#u*-values = Rsi * fkonv * U
#internal Walls 
u_i=0.13 * 2.5/(0.13 + 0.02/1.20 + 0.10/0.05 + 0.02 /1.20 + 0.13)
A_i=(6+5)*2.6
#external Walls
u_e=0.13 * 2.5/(0.13 + 0.20/1.00 + 0.10/0.05 + 0.04)
A_e=(6+5)*2.6
#floor
u_f=0.10 * 5/(0.10 + 0.06/1.20 + 0.10/0.05 +0.20/1.00 + 0.04)
A_f=(6*5)
#ceilling
u_c=0.10 * 0.7/(0.10 + 0.20/1.00 +  0.10)
A_c=(6*5)
#radiator hc=2.5 // Assumption: vertical surfaces dominate
u_r=2.5
A_r = 1 * 1 * 2 + 0.3 * 1  * 2 + 1 * 0.3 * 2

nodes = []
nodes.append(node(1,[1,0]))

boundaries = []
#internal Walls 
boundaries.append(boundary(1,20,u_i*A_i))
#external Walls
boundaries.append(boundary(1,-10,u_e*A_e))
#floor
boundaries.append(boundary(1,5,u_f*A_f))
#ceilling
boundaries.append(boundary(1,20,u_c*A_c))
#radiator
boundaries.append(boundary(1,55,u_r*A_r))

conductions = []

# reference number of nodes to increase readability
n = nodes[0].n

# Initialising conductivity matrix K
K = np.zeros((n,n))

# Initialising boundary vector I0
I0 = np.zeros((n))

# iterater to keep track of cell numbers
for node in nodes:
    #initialize empty conductivity matrix for the current layer
    I0i=0
    Ki=0

    #Test for connected Boundaries, Sources and Nodes
    for boundary in boundaries:
        if boundary.toNode == node.number:
            Ki += boundary.K
            I0i += boundary.K*boundary.temp

            
    for conduction in conductions:
        if conduction.toNode == node.number or conduction.fromNode == node.number:
            Ki += conduction.K
            
    for conduction in conductions:
        if conduction.toNode == node.number:
            K[node.number-1, conduction.fromNode-1]= conduction.K
            K[conduction.fromNode-1, node.number-1]= conduction.K
   
    #defining the diagonal axis
    K[node.number-1, node.number-1]= - Ki
    
    #defining the I0
    I0[node.number-1]= I0i

Calculating the mean air temperature#

T=np.linalg.solve(K,-I0)

Convergence#

After 23 600 timesteps, the above-defined convergence criteria have not been reached. A comparison of the simplified analytical solution and the numerical solution is shown in Fig. 9. It is to be mentioned that the analytic solution is sensitive in regard to the convective heat transfer coefficient and the given range for inside climate (\(2.5\) to \(5 W/m^2K\) depending on surface orientation) has a significant impact on resulting mean air temperature. A possible reason for the numerical instabilities in Fig. 9 could be too low relaxiation factors.

Temperature and velocity field#

Fig. 10 and Fig. 11 cross-sections of the temperature field at timestep \(t=23600\) are shown.Fig. 12 shows a visualization of the air paths passing through points above the radiator. Both the velocity and the temperature field show the effect of natural convection and the resulting thermal circulation in the room. As a result of the buoyancy, the colder soil temperature, and the lower thermal resistance of the floor, a gradient in the air temperature from the floor (18.03°C) to the ceiling (26.27°C) can be observed.

Comfort#

Fig. 13 and Fig. 15 show the impact of the air temperature gradient on the thermal comfort in the room, with a PPD of \(75\%\) above the floor and a PPD of \(10\%\) below the ceiling. Fig. 13 and Fig. 15 show a more homogenous distribution in the x and y directions. At a height of 1.5 m, the PPD ranges between \(5\%\) and \(30\%\).
