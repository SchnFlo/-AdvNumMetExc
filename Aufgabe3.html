Exercise 4 - Coupled Simulation of latent heat effects#

This script is an extension of the script elaborated in Exercise 1. It aims to calculate the temperature and moisture field across a multi-layered building component. Such a multi-layered building component is shown in fig:multilayCompEx2.

In difference to the version shown in Exercise 1 the following restrictions are introduced to reduce the complexity of the coupled problem:

  • the script is only applicable to mineral wool

  • both density and heatcapacity are assumed constant

  • heat conductivity and vapor diffusion coefficient are described by a linear relation

Balance Equations#

To consider the latent heat effect the time derivative of the Temperaturefield seen in (1) is expandend by the moisture induced pressure field. The equation for heat conduction including latent heat is shown in ():

(14)#\[\rho \cdot c \cdot \frac{dT}{dt} = \nabla (\lambda \nabla T + L_v \delta_v \nabla p_{vap}) \]

The introduced moisture field is defined by the balance euqation for moisture transport ():

(15)#\[ \frac{dw}{dt} = \nabla (\delta_v \nabla p_{vap}) \]

Space discretisation#

The discretisation of {eq}gl:heatmoist and {eq}gl:moist is done similar to section Exercise 1 using the finite volume method and a representation as RC-Networks. Evaluating for a cell P surrounded by a cell to the east and west results in () and ():

(18)#\[\rho \cdot c \cdot \frac{T^{n+1}_P - T^n_P }{\Delta t} = \frac{\lambda \cdot ( T^n_E - 2T^n_P + T^n_W) }{\Delta x^2} + \frac{L_v \cdot \delta_v \cdot ( p_{vap,E}^n - 2 p_{vap,P}^n + p_{vap,W}^n) }{\Delta x^2} \]
(17)#\[\frac{w_{P}^{n+1} - w_{P}^n }{\Delta t} =\frac{\delta_v \cdot ( p_{vap,E}^n - 2 p_{vap,P}^n + p_{vap,W}^n) }{\Delta x^2} \]

or written in matrix notation:

(18)#\[\rho \cdot c \cdot \frac{T^{n+1}_P - T^n_P }{\Delta t} = \lambda \cdot K \cdot T + L_v \cdot \delta_v \cdot M \cdot p_{vap} \]

Unlike in the version shown in Exercise 1 the conductivity is not included in the \(K\) matrix. This leads to a constant conductivity matrix, at the cost of an error at the surface resistance. The conductivity matrix K can therefore be written as follows:

\[\begin{split} K = \left [ \begin{array}{ccccccc} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & (...) & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ K_{BC,1} & -(K_{BC,1} + \frac{1}{\Delta x^2}) & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & 0 & (...) & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & -\frac{2}{\Delta x^2} & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & (...) & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...)\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 &(...) & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & -\frac{2}{\Delta x^2} & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 &(...) & 0 & 1 & -(\frac{1}{\Delta x^2} + K_{BC,2}) & K_{BC,2} \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 &(...) & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \end{array} \right] \end{split}\]

And for Cells at the boundary:

\[ K_{BC,1} = K_{0.5} = \frac{1}{R_{s,1} \cdot \Delta x \cdot \lambda + 0.5 \cdot \Delta x^2} \]

To allow a consturction on \(K\) outside of the integrator, \(\lambda_1\) is constant. This results in the above mentioned error. \(M\) is calculated the in the same way as:

\[\begin{split} M = \left [ \begin{array}{ccccccc} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & (...) & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ M_{BC,1} & -(M_{BC,1} + \frac{1}{\Delta x^2}) & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & 0 & (...) & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & -\frac{2}{\Delta x^2} & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & (...) & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0\\ (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...) & (...)\\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 &(...) & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & -\frac{2}{\Delta x^2} & \frac{1}{\Delta x^2} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 &(...) & 0 & 1 & -(\frac{1}{\Delta x^2} + M_{BC,2}) & M_{BC,2} \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 &(...) & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \end{array} \right] \end{split}\]
\[ K_{BC,1} = K_{0.5} = \frac{1}{h_{v} \cdot \Delta x \cdot \delta_v + 0.5 \cdot \Delta x^2} \]

with the surface mass transfer coefficient \(h_v = 3 \cdot 10^{-8}\ m/s\) [4].

def fc_psat_T (T):
    #    return 610.5 * np.exp( 17.269*T / (237.3+T)) # T>0
    #    return 610.5 * np.exp( 21.875*T / (265.5+T)) # T<0
    return np.power(10, 7.625*T/(241+T)+2.7877) # Lehrbuch

def fc_deltapv_T (w,T):
    a= (22.2 + 0.14*T) * 10**-6
    b= -a 
    return a+b*w 

def fc_lambda_w (w):
    return 0.04 + 6*w

def update_Cm(w,phi,T):
	epsilon=0.001*np.min(w)
	wp=w+epsilon
	phip=fc_phi_w(wp)
	dw=abs(wp-w)
	dphi=abs(phip-phi)/100
	pvs=fc_psat_T(T)
	Cm=dw/dphi/pvs
	return Cm

Construction of the conductivity matrix \(K\) and \(M\)#

# reference number of cells to increase readability
n = layer.n

# Initialising conductivity matrix K and M
K = np.zeros((n,n))

# iterater to keep track of cell numbers
counter = 1
for layer in layers:
    #step size for the current layer
    deltaX=layer.width / layer.n_cells

    #initialize empty conductivity matrix for the current layer
    K_current = np.zeros((n,n))

    #defining the "east" and "west" conductivity for interior cells
    K_current[range(counter+1,counter+layer.n_cells),range(counter,counter+layer.n_cells-1)]= 1 / deltaX**2
    K_current[range(counter,counter+layer.n_cells-1),range(counter+1,counter+layer.n_cells)]= 1 / deltaX**2
    
    #defining the "east" and "west" conductivity for the last and first cell of each layer
    #these are added up between layers, thus half the cell distance and inverted
    K_current[(counter,counter+layer.n_cells),(counter-1,counter+layer.n_cells-1)]= 1 / 2 *   deltaX**2
    K_current[(counter-1,counter+layer.n_cells-1),(counter,counter+layer.n_cells)]= 1/ 2  * deltaX**2
    
    K += K_current
    
    #Inverting the Konductivity between surfaces after adding them
    K[(counter),(counter-1)] = 1/K[(counter),(counter-1)]
    K[(counter-1),(counter)] = 1/K[(counter-1),(counter)]

        
    counter += layer.n_cells

# Initialising M as a copy of K
M=copy.deepcopy(K)

# Boundary Conditions for K
deltaX_l = (layers[0].width / layers[0].n_cells)
deltaX_r = (layers[-1].width / layers[-1].n_cells)
K_l =  1/(boundaries.resistance1 *  layers[0].thermConduct * deltaX_l+ 0.5 *deltaX_l**2)
K_r =  1/(boundaries.resistance2 * layers[-1].thermConduct *deltaX_r+ 0.5 *deltaX_r**2)
K[1,0] = K_l
K[len(K)-2,len(K)-1] = K_r

# Boundary Conditions for M
deltapv_l=fc_deltapv_T(fc_w_phi(boundaries.humidity1),boundaries.temperature1 )
deltapv_r=fc_deltapv_T(fc_w_phi(boundaries.humidity2),boundaries.temperature2 )
M_l =  1/(2 *deltaX_l**2)
M_r =  1/(2 *deltaX_r**2)
M[1,0] = M_l
M[len(K)-2,len(K)-1] = M_r

#The diagonal of the conductivity matrix is defined as the negative sum of the 
#"east" and "west" conductivity for the cell
K[range(0,len(K)),range(0,len(K))]= -np.sum(K,axis=1)
M[range(0,len(M)),range(0,len(M))]= -np.sum(M,axis=1)

#free first and last row; this leads to constant values at the boundaries
K[0,:]=0
K[-1,:]=0

M[0,:]=0
M[-1,:]=0

Variant 2 - Coupled Simulation#

def dt_w_dt(t,vec_T_w):
    #Splitting up temp and water vector
    T = vec_T_w[0:layer.n]
    w = vec_T_w[layer.n:layer.n*2]
    
    c = 1000
    rho = 160
    Lv = 2503000
    
    phi = fc_phi_w(w)
    thermConduct = fc_lambda_w(w)
    deltapv = fc_deltapv_T(w,T)
    
    
    
    pvap = fc_psat_T(T) * phi /100
    
    delta_T =  (thermConduct * np.dot(K,T) +  deltapv * Lv * np.dot(M,pvap) )/(c*rho)
    
    delta_w =  deltapv *  np.dot(M,pvap) 
    
    return np.append(delta_T,delta_w)

result_coupled = scipy.integrate.solve_ivp(dt_w_dt,(0,1800000),vec_T_w,method="Radau",atol=1e-7,rtol=1e-5)

res_T_heat=[]
